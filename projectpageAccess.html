Teaching Accessibility
2014-2016

Beginning in April 2014, I worked with professors at DePaul University in Chicago and the University of Washington on a project about teaching accessibility. Our paper, Best Practices for Teaching Accessibility in University Classrooms: Cultivating Awareness, Understanding and Appreciation for Diverse Users was therefore a cross-geographical endeavour, and resulted in our January 2015 publication in the Transactions on Accessible Computing.

For this project, we contacted computer science teachers the top 160 universities in the United States, then interviewed the 38 who responded about how they integrate accessibility topics into the program or their particular class.

In addition to how they taught accessibility, we spoke with interviewees about ways that they get their students to care about accessibility, as well as pitfalls in teaching accessibility.

Research Flow Chart

Using Omnigraffle, I created a research flow chart for our team to detail what the protocol is for each interaction with our contacts; specifically, keywords to search for in the course catalogue and how to handle correspondence from each contact.

Publication

In January 2016 we received notification that our paper will be published in the Transactions on Accessible Computing, a quarterly journal of the Association for Computing Machinery (ACM).

Analysis

Transcriptions were put into Atlas.ti, an excellent qualitative data analysis tool. I performed an inductive analysis/qualitative coding, creating themes which were then entered into a codebook. Other members of the team then marked the transcriptions with what they considered the appropriate code. We limited our themes and sub-categories only to those which met Cohen's Kappa rate as a mark for a "good" agreement.
